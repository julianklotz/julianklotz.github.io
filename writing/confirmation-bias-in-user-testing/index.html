    Evaluator Bias in UX

    Mar 1, 2023

    Evaluator Bias in UX

    In one of my first jobs, I found myself in a situation being the only person in the company trained in UX design. To get an idea of the how the company’s products worked, I did several rounds of user testing. Users are assigned certain tasks that they should fulfill using your product. By asking people to articulate their thoughts and decisions, you quickly get an idea of what works and what doesn’t. Following Steve Krug’s advice in Rocket Surgery Made Easy, I made it a “spectator sport” and asked all kinds of people from the company to watch the sessions in a remote room. As the sessions gained more and more interest, others in my team realized how valuable the insights were and we started co-moderating sessions.

    We mostly agreed on the problems we identified, like: “The participant had difficulty finding the relevant button at the bottom right of the screen” . Although participants articulated their thoughts and motivation, we often disagreed on the causes — or the why — of our observations. Was the typography too small, the color contrast too low, or the button in completely the wrong place? Moreover, our assessments of the relevance of the problems differed vastly.

    Cause & Effect

    All product designers I’ve worked with, including myself, have a wishlist of things to change, at least in their heads. The wishes sometimes come from observations in the field (lovely!), but more often, they root from personal preferences, aesthetics and other assumptions. Observations in testing sessions that support our assumptions feel more serious and relevant than others. This effect is commonly called Confirmation Bias or, more specifically, evaluator bias. Despite best intentions, this effect distorts our findings and compromises the neutrality that user testing brings to the table.

    Gradually, I learned to appreciate discussing the reasons for the observations we made with other evaluators after the sessions. Now, I believe that this part is just as important as the testing sessions themselves.

    Avoiding Evaluator Bias

    A few mitigation strategies:

    • During the sessions, ask as much as possible what the causes of the observed behavior are, especially if participants do not articulate related thoughts on their own
    • A team should conduct the evaluation, rather than an individual
    • The team should be aware of the severe consequences of evaulator bias and be able to accurately differentiate between personal assumptions and observations from real-life behavior
